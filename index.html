PROJECTS

Network Role Discovery and Node Importance

This project aims to analyze the Bitcoin OTC trust network to distinguish key players and potentially risky users through the discovery of node roles and their relative importance.

In this project, I analyzed the Bitcoin over-the-counter (OTC) trust network to understand how trust flows between traders and identify both key players and potentially risky users. Each user and their connections were transformed into numerical patterns, called embeddings. Embeddings were made using two complementary techniques: Node2Vec, which classifies users based on their direct connections, and Struc2Vec, which identifies users who occupy similar roles in the network, even if they aren't directly linked.

By grouping these embeddings into separate clusters, I uncovered distinct structural roles, revealing patterns of behavior and influence across the network. To further understand each user's importance, I applied three centrality measures: Eigenvector Centrality to find users connected to other influential users, Degree Centrality to locate the most connected users, and Closeness Centrality to highlight those who can quickly reach others.

This combination of network embedding, clustering, and centrality analysis allowed me to map the network's structure, pinpoint influential participants, and identify potential risks, providing a clear, data-driven picture of trust dynamics in the Bitcoin OTC ecosystem.

Wildfire Risk Assessment: Predicting West Coast Wildfire Size and Severity

This project aims to analyze historical wildfire, weather, and population data to predict potential fire size and severity using key environmental and demographic factors.

In this project, I analyzed historical wildfire, weather, and population data across the West Coast of the United States to predict wildfire risk and estimate potential fire size and severity. Each wildfire incident was transformed into a structured feature vector capturing environmental variables, climatic conditions, and local demographic information.

These feature vectors were used as input to a Random Forest model, which creates an ensemble of decision trees to identify complex, non-linear relationships between variables and predict whether a fire is likely to exceed a certain size. Feature importance analysis further highlighted the most influential factors, such as temperature extremes, vapor pressure deficit, precipitation, and population density.

This combination of predictive modeling and feature analysis enabled the identification of high-risk areas, provided insights into the key drivers of wildfire severity, and offered a data-driven foundation for proactive risk mitigation.
